Step 1 of 6

  1. Square a line around the duct at each end of the middle piece.
  2. From each line, measure the layout mark across the cheek — top corner to bottom corner — and strike the diagonal. Both diagonals lean the same way.
  3. Cut both lines, all four sides.
  4. Flip the middle piece end for end. Don't rotate it — flip it like closing a book.
  5. Fit it back in. The duct now steps over by your offset.

Offsets. Cut joint = travel − both tangent lengths, where travel is offset ÷ sin(angle) and each tangent is the elbow centerline radius × tan(half the angle). Centerline radius is throat + half the duct; a mitered ell has no radius, so the cut joint is the whole travel. Rolling offsets use the true offset — the square root of sideways² + up² — and the roll angle is what you rotate the fitting to. Everything is centerline; add your own seam and joint allowance.

Friction rate is your number, not the material's. It comes from the fan's available static divided by the run length — 0.08 is 0.08 whether it's metal, board or flex. What the material changes is the size you need to hit that rate, which is what the line under Duct type shows you. Metal uses the ASHRAE friction chart equation, same as the dial. Ductboard and flex use Darcy–Weisbach with Colebrook–White at 0.003 and 0.010 ft roughness. Square and round convert on equal friction (Huebscher), exact, never rounded to stock — round up to what you can buy.
